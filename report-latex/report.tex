%%%%%%%%% ABSTRACT
\begin{abstract}

Can personal safety be treated as a primary objective in pedestrian route planning, rather than an afterthought to distance and time? Mainstream running and navigation apps optimise almost entirely for speed, leaving solo runners, especially after dark, to judge safety for themselves. This project addresses that gap by building and deploying a safety-first, lighting-aware routing tool for the London Borough of Camden. 

On the data side, open records of crime, stop-and-search, and street lighting are attached to an OpenStreetMap street graph at the level of individual streets. Each crime is scored in two complementary ways, by legal severity and by perceived risk, drawing on the Cambridge Crime Harm Index and the Signal Crimes Perspective, and its influence is spread along the walkable network using a Network-Based Kernel Density Estimate that respects the anonymised resolution of the police data.

After processing, the data is passed through an XGBoost Regressor using spatial-temporal lags and infrastructure metadata received to forecast (perceived) street-level risk. The model employs Tweedie deviance for a zero-inflated, right-skewed distribution seen in this localisation. These continuous values are then scaled and integrated with lighting penalties to be fed into the pathfinding algorithm for optimal, safe routing. 

In a Monte Carlo evaluation of 1,000 random origin-destination pairs across Camden, the recommended route diverged from the shortest path in 98.5\% of cases. Daytime routing reduced crime-risk exposure by 80.46\% for a 7.32\% increase in distance, while night-time routing, which additionally penalises unlit streets, cut darkness exposure by 53.35\% at the cost of a larger 23.15\% detour. The underlying predictive model achieved a mean absolute error of 2.46 on held-out future months.

The result is a working prototype showing that data-driven safety routing is feasible and practically useful for everyday runners.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Running participation in the UK has grown steadily in recent years, and events such as the London Marathon now attract record numbers of applicants. Running is one of the most accessible forms of exercise, requiring no gym membership or specialist equipment, and is widely recognised for its mental health benefits alongside its physical ones. Existing running and journey-planning apps, however, optimise almost exclusively for distance or time. Neither category of app treats personal safety as a crucial routing objective, despite safety being a commonly cited concern for solo runners, after dark or in unfamiliar cities.

This project addresses that gap by building a safety-first and street-lighting-aware pedestrian routing tool, scoped to the London Borough of Camden as a case study. The system attaches risk-related features derived from open crime, stop-and-search, and street lighting data to a street-level graph, trains a predictive model on that history. The resulting risk estimates are injected directly into a production-grade routing engine (Valhalla), alongside current streetlight coverage information, so that riskier streets are naturally avoided where a reasonable alternative exists. Two separate indicators for safety and lighting are shown alongside each route for the user's own judgement.

The remainder of this report describes prior work in safety-aware routing (Section 2), the data pipeline, predictive model, and routing integration that were actually built (Section 3), the datasets, software, and hardware involved (Section 4), the current state of evaluation together with an honest account of what remains outstanding against the project methodology (Section 5), and finally our conclusions, acknowledgements and contributions (Sections 6-8).

\section{Related Work}

A small but growing body of work treats safety as an explicit navigation objective rather than a secondary constraint. Atterkar et al.~\cite{atterkar2025womensafe} propose a Women Safe Route Navigation System that scores streets by risk and steers users away from higher-risk areas. Harideviprathap et al.~\cite{harideviprathap2026mogat} extend this with Mo-GAT, a multi-objective spatiotemporal graph attention network that predicts safety risk across a street network and adapts as conditions change over space and time. Anson~\cite{anson2025mlrouting} studies how a machine-learned weighting of a road network can be consumed by a path-finding algorithm in a smart urban transport setting, which is architecturally close to the approach this project ultimately takes. Tang et al.~\cite{tang2024itinera} pair spatial optimisation with large language models to plan open-ended urban itineraries, demonstrating that data-driven, objective-aware routing is achievable at city scale.

These studies establish that safety-first, data-driven routing is both feasible and an active research area, but each targets either a specific demographic-focused safety application or a general urban commuting/itinerary-planning use case. None specifically targets the everyday runner, and none of the mainstream commercial running or journey-planning applications currently used by the public (Section 1) incorporate a safety objective at all. This project sits between these two spaces: applying a data-driven safety model, in the spirit of \cite{atterkar2025womensafe,harideviprathap2026mogat}, but integrated directly into a general-purpose, open-source routing engine rather than a bespoke path-finding implementation.

\section{Proposed Method}

\subsection{Data Pipeline}

The first stage is a data pipeline that turns raw open data into a table with one row per street segment. A pedestrian street graph for Camden is downloaded from OpenStreetMap via OSMnx~\cite{boeing2017osmnx}, giving 35{,}532 edges. Onto it we attach three open datasets at edge level: recorded crime and stop-and-search from the UK Police API~\cite{datapoliceuk2026}, and street lighting from Camden Council~\cite{camden2026lighting}. Crime is pulled month by month; since data.police.uk only keeps a rolling window, the pipeline clips the request to whatever is actually published at run time, here 36 months from June 2023 to May 2026.

We compute two crime weightings per incident, since ``serious'' and ``frightening'' differ. A \emph{severity} score (1--3) follows the Cambridge Crime Harm Index~\cite{sherman2016cambridge} and the ONS Crime Severity Score~\cite{ons2016severity}; a separate \emph{perceived risk} score (1--4) follows the Signal Crimes Perspective~\cite{innes2004signal,innesfielding2002signal} and ONS survey data~\cite{ons2022csew}, under which visible disorder can outweigh a legally more serious but less visible offence. Each edge's score is the sum of its incidents' weights. Both are deliberate simplifications onto the 14 categories the API exposes, not a full reproduction of either study. Only the perceived risk weighting is formalised below and carried into the model; the severity score is computed and retained in the dataset but is not used as a model feature.
\begin{equation*}
w_c = \begin{cases} 
4 & \parbox[t]{6cm}{\raggedright Violence, Robbery, Weapons, Theft from Person (Direct Threats)} \\[3ex]
3 & \parbox[t]{6cm}{\raggedright Anti-Social Behaviour, Public Order, Criminal Damage, Drugs (Signal Crimes)} \\[3ex]
2 & \parbox[t]{6cm}{\raggedright Burglary, Vehicle Crime, Bicycle Theft (Property Crimes)} \\[2ex]
1 & \parbox[t]{6cm}{\raggedright Shoplifting, Other Theft, Other Crimes (Low Visual Signal)}
\end{cases}
\end{equation*}

For an edge $e$ in month $t$, the unadjusted discrete perceived risk sum $R_{e, t}^{\text{raw}}$ is:

\begin{equation*}
R_{e, t}^{\text{raw}} = \sum_{i \in I_{e,t}} w_{c(i)}
\end{equation*}

Police coordinates are anonymised to fixed snap points roughly 125\,m apart in Camden, so assigning a crime to one nearest street would be misleadingly precise. Instead, each incident's weight is spread over nearby streets using Network Kernel Density Estimation (NetKDE; formalised in Section~\ref{sec:netkde})~\cite{chainey2008hotspot}, with distance measured \emph{along} the walkable graph (Dijkstra shortest paths) rather than in a straight line, so a crime cannot bleed across a wall or the canal. The bandwidth is set to 125\,m to match the anonymisation spacing, and smoothing is done separately per month so future data cannot leak into earlier months.

The pipeline outputs two tables. \texttt{edge\_features.csv} holds the crime and stop-and-search features (overall totals, a per-month breakdown, and the per-month NetKDE columns used by the model); \texttt{edge\_lamp\_features.csv} holds the lighting features separately, as lighting is a current snapshot with no monthly history. Both carry the OpenStreetMap way ID and endpoint coordinates, so any row traces back to a real place. After cleaning, the dataset holds 230{,}707 crimes, 24{,}644 stop-and-search records, and 15{,}561 lamps. Stop-and-search gender, age, and ethnicity fields were deliberately excluded to avoid encoding demographic bias into a safety score.

\subsection{Predictive Safety Model}

\subsubsection{Problem Formulation}

The objective of this predictive safety routing system is to compute an optimal and safe path for pedestrians and runners operating at night. Urban street network is modelled as a weighted directed graph $G = (V, E)$, where $V$ represents street intersections (nodes), $E$ represents street segments (edges) ~\cite{LozanoDominguez2021}. Each edge $e = (u, v) \in E$ has a street length $l_e$, spatial geometry, static street infrastructure, and time-series historical crime metrics aggregated at monthly intervals $t \in T$. 

The system separates future crime risk prediction from graph pathfinding by utilising an \textbf{XGBoost Regressor} that estimates future spatial-temporal risk $y_{e, t}$ per street segment. These are then transformed to normalised edge costs $C_e \in [1, 10]$ and integrated into a dynamic graph function that accounts for environmental factors to determine the optimal path via Djistra search algorithm. 

\subsubsection{Network Kernel Density Estimation (NetKDE)}
\label{sec:netkde}

NetKDE was chosen in order to model risk diffusion along actual pedestrian pathways rather than Euclidean space, while eliminating artefacts caused by spatial snapping~\cite{Wu2022}. For each incident $i$ on edge $e$, risk diffuses in walkable paths using Dijkstra's shortest-path distance $d_{\text{net}}(e, e')$ with a Gaussian kernel function $K(d_{\text{net}})$ bounded by a distance $B = 125\text{ m}$. 
\begin{align*}
K(d_{\text{net}}) &= \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{d_{\text{net}}}{\sigma} \right)^2 \right), \\
&\quad \text{where } \sigma = \frac{B}{3} = \frac{125}{3} \approx 41.67\text{ m}
\end{align*}

Continuous training label $y_{e, t}$ for edge $e$ in month $t$ is computed monthly so that we prevent data leakage. 

\begin{equation*}
y_{e, t} = \text{target\_perceived\_risk}_{e,t} = \sum_{e' \in \mathcal{N}_B(e)} R_{e', t}^{\text{raw}} \cdot K(d_{\text{net}}(e, e'))
\end{equation*}

\subsubsection{Feature Engineering}

As UK police API is subject to a 2-month lag, the predictive feature strictly omits real-time figures. Input feature vector $\mathbf{x}_{e, t}$ for edge $e$ at prediction time $t$ is as follows: 

\begin{align*}
\mathbf{x}_{e, t} = \Big[ &L_2(y_{e,t}), L_3(y_{e,t}), L_4(y_{e,t}), L_2(S_{e,t}), \\
&L_3(S_{e,t}), L_4(S_{e,t}), l_e, \text{type}_e, \text{lat}_e, \\
&\text{lng}_e, \rho_{\text{lamp}}, \text{lit}_e \Big]^T
\end{align*}

Where:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Historical Lags ($L_k$):} $L_k(y_{e,t}) = y_{e, t-k}$ represents the NetKDE perceived risk score $k$ months prior ($k \in \{2, 3, 4\}$).
    \item \textbf{Stop and Search Lags ($S_{e, t-k}$):} $L_k(S_{e,t}) = S_{\text{NetKDE}, e, t-k}$ represents 2, 3, and 4-month lagged NetKDE stop-and-search counts, acting as a proxy for active police presence.
    \item \textbf{Geometric \& Topological Features:} Edge length $l_e$, highway classification category $\text{type}_e$ (e.g., residential, footway, primary), and endpoint node coordinates $(u_{\text{lat}}, u_{\text{lng}}, v_{\text{lat}}, v_{\text{lng}})$.
    \item \textbf{Lighting Infrastructure Features:} Lamp post spatial density $\rho_{\text{lamp}} = \frac{\text{lamp\_count}_e}{l_e / 1000}$ (lamps per km) and binary OSM/municipal lighting flag $\text{lit}_e \in \{0, 1\}$.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{XGBoost Regressor}

XGBoost was selected over alternative models mainly due to three main reasons:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Tree-based gradient boosting outperforms GNNs and deep learning architectures on heterogeneous tabular datasets with dense scalar lag features~\cite{Wu2022}.
    \item Native support for categorical features via numerical splits without expanding feature dimensionality.
    \item Fast prediction latency enabling faster scoring if more frequent data were to be obtained. 
\end{enumerate}

The model fits an ensemble of $K$ additive decision trees:

\begin{equation*}
\mathcal{L}(\Theta) = \sum_{e, t} d\left( y_{e,t}, \hat{y}_{e,t} \right) + \sum_{k=1}^K \Omega(f_k)
\end{equation*}

where $d$ is the loss function \texttt{reg:tweedie}, a distribution defined by $Var(Y) = \phi \mu^p$ where $p$ is variance power parameter. As $p$ is constrained to  $1 < p < 2$, this turns the model into a compound Poisson-Gamma distribution which handles zero-inflated data and positive tails. Tweedie unit deviance is as follows: 

$$d(y, \hat{y}) = 2 \left( \frac{y \hat{y}^{1-p}}{1-p} - \frac{\hat{y}^{2-p}}{2-p} - \frac{y^{2-p}}{(1-p)(2-p)} \right)$$

Regularisation term  $\Omega(f_k) = \gamma T_k + \frac{1}{2}\lambda \Vert{}w_k\Vert{}^2$ penalises tree complexity, which then prevents spatial overfitting and results in better, more conservative splits. 

\subsubsection{Data Split Strategy}

To evaluate model generalisation without any leakages, a chronological split is enforced:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Training Set:} Historical records prior to March 2026.
    \item \textbf{Test Set:} Historical records from March 2026 onwards.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Hyperparameter Configuration}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Number of Trees:} 500 (\texttt{n\_estimators}).
    \item \textbf{Learning Rate:} 0.05.
    \item \textbf{Tree Depth:} Maximum depth of 6.
    \item \textbf{Sampling:} Both \texttt{subsample} and \texttt{colsample\_bytree} are set to 0.8 to prevent overfitting.
    \item \textbf{Categorical Handling:} Natively enabled (\texttt{enable\_categorical=True}) for features like highway types.
    \item \textbf{Early Stopping:} Set to 50 rounds to halt training if validation performance stops improving.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Training \& Evaluation Strategy}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Chronological Split:} The dataset is split sequentially, using a cutoff month (March 2026) to train on past historical data and test strictly on future data.
    \item \textbf{Data Masking:} Raw identifiers (e.g., \texttt{osmid}, \texttt{edge\_id}, \texttt{name}) and current-month crime totals are dropped to prevent the model from learning raw IDs or experiencing target leakage.
    \item \textbf{Evaluation Metric:} The model's predictive accuracy is evaluated using Mean Absolute Error (MAE) on the future test set.
    \item \textbf{Interpretability:} Feature importance is calculated and plotted using ``Information Gain'' to determine which variables have the highest predictive power. (See Appendix \ref{app:evaluation})
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Routing Integration}
\label{sec:routing-integration}

Rather than implementing a custom path-finding algorithm, the risk tags produced by the model and the raw lighting dataset are converted into cost multipliers and supplied to Valhalla~\cite{valhalla2026}, an open-source routing engine, via its native \texttt{linear\_cost\_factors} request parameter. For a street edge with risk tier $\text{tag} \in \{1, \ldots, 11\}$ and lit status $\text{lit} \in \{0, 1\}$, the two multipliers are

\begin{equation*}
\text{safety\_factor}(\text{tag}) = 1 + \text{SCALE}_{\text{safety}} \cdot \max(0, \text{tag} - 1)
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
\text{lighting\_factor}(\text{lit}) =
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if lit} = 1 \\
1 + \text{SCALE}_{\text{light}} & \text{if lit} = 0
\end{cases}
\end{equation*}

with $\text{SCALE}_{\text{safety}} = 3$ and $\text{SCALE}_{\text{light}} = 2$, tunable constants. The two combine multiplicatively into the single factor submitted per edge, with the lighting term collapsing to 1 whenever a request is flagged as daylight (\texttt{is\_daylight}, default \texttt{FALSE}):

\begin{equation*}
\text{factor} = \text{safety\_factor}(\text{tag}) \times
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if is\_daylight} \\
\text{lighting\_factor}(\text{lit}) & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation*}

Edges at baseline on both dimensions (factor = 1, the large majority of the network) are omitted from the request entirely, keeping each request small. Valhalla's existing shortest-path search then naturally prefers routes that avoid heavily-penalised streets where a reasonable detour exists, while still permitting a short traversal of a risky or unlit street when no reasonable alternative is available, since the engine is minimising a distorted notion of distance rather than obeying a hard exclusion rule.

\subsubsection*{Safety Score}
A separate, purely descriptive \textbf{safety score} (0-100) is computed \emph{after} routing, by measuring what proportion of a returned route's length passes within a fixed proximity radius of a flagged street; this score has no influence on which route is chosen; it exists to communicate, in an interpretable way, how much residual exposure a given route carries.

\subsubsection*{Lighting Indicator}
A second, independently displayed \textbf{lighting score} (0-100) reports what proportion of a route's length passes near a currently lit street, based directly on the raw lamp dataset rather than a trained forecast. This descriptive measure is computed from proximity to lit streets along the already-chosen route, and, like the safety score, has no influence on which route is chosen or on the automatic ``recommended route'' selection (Section~\ref{sec:routing-engine}), which evaluates only the descriptive safety score. It is a separate signal from the lighting \emph{penalty} defined above: one shapes which route Valhalla proposes, the other simply reports on whichever route was returned.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Dataset}
The street network, crime, stop-and-search, and lighting data are all scoped to the London Borough of Camden, extracted as an OpenStreetMap \texttt{.osm.pbf} file via the BBBike extract service~\cite{bbbike2026}. The dataset spans 36 monthly periods from June 2023 to May 2026, covering 35{,}532 street edges, 230{,}707 crime records, and 24{,}644 stop-and-search records. Records with missing or non-numeric coordinates and exact duplicate rows are removed during cleaning, and all coordinates are reprojected to the British National Grid to support metre-based distance calculations.

\subsection{Software}
The data pipeline uses Python with OSMnx~\cite{boeing2017osmnx}, GeoPandas, Shapely, pandas, and pyproj. The predictive model uses XGBoost. The routing engine is Valhalla~\cite{valhalla2026}, run inside Docker alongside a Python (FastAPI) proxy service that injects the safety cost factors into each routing request and computes the descriptive safety and lighting scores. The frontend is a React/MapLibre web application (adapted from Valhalla's own reference web app) presenting routes, scores, and turn-by-turn directions to the user.

\section{Results and Discussion}
\subsection{XGBoost Performance}

Training using 1,030,544 edges monthly spanning prior to March 2026, and testing using 106,608 unseen records from March 2026 onwards, the model achieved an MAE of 2.46. Within the context of a continuous spatial-temporal NetKDE perceived risk score this MAE indicates the model can reliably estimate future perceived risk within a tight margin. 

Analysis of the Information Gain reveals that most recent spatial-temporal lags are the most powerful predictors of future crime. The 2-month, 3-month and 5-month NetKDE risk lags provide the highest power, followed by street categorisation, geographic coordinates and edge length. Interestingly, the stop-search density ranked higher than lighting features. 

\subsection{Routing Outcomes}

To evaluate whether the model was indeed usable, effective and successful in the main objective as opposed to just the predictive element, the algorithm was subjected to a Monte Carlo simulation of 1000 random Origin-Destination pairs across the network. Dijkstra's algorithm was compared against ML algorithms with Daytime and Nighttime algorithms (the latter applying a 5 impedance penalty to unlit streets)

98.5\% of the 1000 valid simulations actively diverged from the shortest path, proving the algorithm works at a baseline of deviating and affecting the total path. (see Appendix \ref{app:data_tables} for further metrics). 

\subsection{Lighting vs Crime Trade-off}

Night-time routing introduces another objective optimisation problem; due to the dark penalty ($\delta = 5.0$), the night-time routing achieved a 53.35\% reduction in darkness exposure. However, this comes at a cost of a detour penalty jumping to 23.15\%, and a crime risk reduction metric dropping from 80.46\% to 63.72\%(see Appendix \ref{app:data_tables}). This drop makes logical sense, as well-lit streets (commercial and nightlife districts) may sometimes suffer from higher base crime rates. The risk reduction is still very sizeable, and this represents a compromise to ensure that routing is significantly safer in both the perceived and physical sense. 

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Temporal \& Spatial Aggregation:} UK Police API is subject to a 2-month publication delay, forcing the model to rely on a $t-2$ lag. Adding to this, police crime coordinates are anonymized every ~125m interval, therefore NetKDE smoothing resolves this but leaves the data with artificial peaks. 
    \item \textbf{Subjectivity:}Transformation of raw crime into 1-4 perceived risk categorical index relies on generalized heuristics. Human perception of safety is subjective and non-linear, varying drastically per user. 
    \item \textbf{Lighting Limitations:} \texttt{is\_lit} environmental variable currently treats streets as a static binary state. It does not account for real-time infrastructure failures or lumen count variation based on any other number of environmental variables. 
    \item \textbf{Static Time-of-Day:} Currently the switch is on a binary toggle, a more granular time-dependent transitional weighting during dusk and dawn would fit better, but also be computationally prohibitive. 
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Routing Engine}
\label{sec:routing-engine}

The frontend is a React/MapLibre application adapted from Valhalla's reference web app, presenting each returned route as a tile showing distance, time, a descriptive safety score, and a descriptive lighting score, with the same information available on hover over the route line itself. A user-facing day/night toggle exists to simulate responsive behaviour depending on the time of day, setting the \texttt{is\_daylight} flag described in Section~\ref{sec:routing-integration}; it defaults to \texttt{FALSE}, simulating user behaviour after dark.

In practice, the SCALE constants (Section~\ref{sec:routing-integration}) control a genuine trade-off: a street with a 4$\times$ safety multiplier is treated as roughly four times its real length, so the engine takes a detour only when a genuinely shorter alternative exists, whereas the highest-risk streets (up to 31$\times$ at tag 11) are avoided far more readily, though the engine will still traverse a short high-risk stretch rather than accept an extremely long detour, since it is minimising a distorted distance rather than applying a hard exclusion. A maximally risky, unlit street is penalised up to roughly 93$\times$ (31 safety $\times$ 3 lighting) when the lighting penalty is active.

A ``recommended route'' selection was also implemented on top of the three routes Valhalla returns per request. By default the fastest route is recommended, but if its descriptive safety score falls below a threshold, the fastest route within a 20\% time budget that clears the threshold is recommended instead.

\section{Conclusions}

\subsection{Goals \& Previous Work}
Against the project proposal's goal of a data-driven, deployable safety router, the evaluation shows the system does more than passively score routes. 98.5\% of simulated journeys actively diverged from the shortest path, and daytime routing achieved an 80.46\% reduction in crime-risk exposure for a modest 7.32\% distance cost, with night-time routing trading a larger detour for a 53.35\% reduction in darkness exposure. This project delivers a working, deployable prototype: a pedestrian router for Camden that prioritises safety, built by attaching a predictive crime-risk model's output and lighting coverage data to a production routing engine's native costing mechanism. The methodology of this particular project advances prior approaches to safety navigation~\cite{Novack2018} by engineering said temporal pipeline that utilizes NetKDE in a practical approach ~\cite{Wu2022}. The system takes signal crime theory ~\cite{Innes2004} and several crime indexes ~\cite{Sherman2016} to output a predictive model that mathematically parametrizes human perceptions of safety to mapped graphs in the real world. 

\subsection{Future Work}

Looking forward, while XGBoost provides highly capable metrics exploring Graph Attention Networks in more detail is the next step forward. More autonomous non-linear relationships could be taken to gain insight to the data and avoid manual lag engineering. Moreover, moving away from binary day/night time routing to live chronological endpoints with a slow gradient approach to the time of day directly tied to level of light could be a more rigorous approach to lighting penalties. Finally, post-widespread adoption crowdsourcing telemetry in order to integrate it directly into the model could provide high-resolution micro-level data that fills the gap for monthly, anonymised crime data. This would enable us to shift from a historical predictive model to a real-time reactive navigation tool and allow us to explore said complex non-linear relationships to their full extent.
